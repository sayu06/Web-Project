Gion Matsuri - July 1-31 (Kyoto)

Gion Matsuri is one of the most famous and important festivals in Japan, held annually in the Gion district of Kyoto during the entire month of July. The festival has a long history, dating back over a thousand years, and is dedicated to the Yasaka Shrine, which is located in the heart of Kyoto.

The festival is known for its colorful and lively parade, called "Yamaboko Junko," which takes place on July 17th and features dozens of elaborately decorated floats, called "Yamaboko," that are pulled through the streets of Kyoto. The parade is accompanied by traditional music and dance performances, as well as groups of people dressed in traditional clothing.


Tenjin Matsuri - July 24-25 (Osaka)

Tenjin Matsuri, also known as the Tenjin Festival, is one of the three major festivals in Japan, held annually on July 24th and 25th in Osaka. The festival is dedicated to Sugawara no Michizane, a scholar and politician who is believed to have become a deity after his death.

The festival features a lively river procession, where decorated boats carrying portable shrines and musicians float down the Okawa River. Spectators can enjoy the colorful display from the banks of the river or from bridges.

Nebuta Matsuri - August 2-7 (Aomori)

Nebuta Matsuri is a famous Japanese festival held annually from August 2nd to 7th in Aomori, a city located in the northern part of Honshu Island in Japan. The festival is known for its spectacular lantern floats, called "nebuta," which are made of paper and bamboo and are illuminated from within.

The festival's highlight is the "nebuta" parade, where large floats depicting historical and mythological figures are pulled through the streets of Aomori by groups of men and women, who dance and chant traditional songs as they move the floats. The parade also features traditional Japanese taiko drumming and other performances.

In addition to the parade, the festival includes various other events such as live music performances, fireworks displays, and a market selling local foods and crafts.


Awa Odori - August 12-15 (Tokushima)

Awa Odori is a traditional Japanese dance festival held annually from August 12th to 15th in Tokushima, a city located on the island of Shikoku in Japan. The festival is known for its energetic and lively dance performances, which are performed by groups of dancers wearing traditional costumes.

The dance performances are held throughout the city, and participants march in a procession along the streets, accompanied by traditional Japanese music played on shamisen, taiko drums, and other instruments. The dance itself is a combination of traditional Japanese movements and modern choreography, and it is performed by both professional dancers and amateurs.


Tanabata Matsuri - July 7 (various cities throughout Japan)

Tanabata Matsuri, also known as the Star Festival, is a traditional Japanese festival celebrated on July 7th each year in various cities throughout Japan. The festival celebrates the meeting of two celestial lovers, Orihime and Hikoboshi, who are represented by the stars Vega and Altair.

The festival typically involves the decoration of bamboo branches with colorful paper decorations and wishes written on small strips of paper called "tanzaku." The bamboo branches are then displayed in public areas such as shopping streets, parks, and temples. During the festival, there are also various events and performances such as traditional Japanese dance and music, as well as food stalls selling local cuisine.


Kanda Matsuri - mid-May (Tokyo)

Kanda Matsuri is a traditional festival held in mid-May in Tokyo, Japan. It is one of the three great festivals of Tokyo and is considered one of the most important festivals in the city. The festival takes place over several days and is dedicated to the Kanda Myojin Shrine, which is located in the Chiyoda ward of Tokyo.

During the festival, participants parade through the streets of Tokyo, carrying portable shrines and other religious objects. The festival is known for its lively atmosphere and colorful displays, as well as the traditional clothing worn by the participants.


Jidai Matsuri - October 22 (Kyoto)

Jidai Matsuri is a traditional festival held on October 22nd every year in Kyoto, Japan. It is one of the three major festivals in Kyoto and is dedicated to celebrating the city's rich cultural heritage and history.

The festival begins with a procession of over 2,000 people dressed in period costumes, representing various historical figures from different eras of Kyoto's history. The procession starts at the Kyoto Imperial Palace and proceeds through the streets of the city to the Heian Jingu Shrine.

The participants in the procession are divided into different groups, each representing a different era of Kyoto's history. The costumes are meticulously crafted to represent the clothing and styles of each period, ranging from the Heian period (794-1185) to the Meiji period (1868-1912).


Kanamara Matsuri - first Sunday in April (Kawasaki)

Kanamara Matsuri, also known as the "Festival of the Steel Phallus," is a traditional Japanese festival that takes place on the first Sunday in April at the Kanayama Shrine in Kawasaki, Japan.

The festival is believed to have started in the Edo period (1603-1867) as a way to pray for protection against sexually transmitted infections and to promote fertility and safe childbirth. The festival also serves as a way to raise awareness about HIV/AIDS and to celebrate diversity and inclusivity.

Overall, Kanamara Matsuri is a unique and colorful celebration of sexuality, diversity, and cultural tradition in Japan.
