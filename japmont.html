Japanese Months

The Japanese months are called simply numbered from 1 to 12, followed by the kanji for month 月. This kanji character also means “moon” on its own.

Japanese Months

English Translation

Pronunciation

いちがつ

January

ichigatsu

The first month of the year is characterized by the most important event in Japan’s calendar, the Japanese New Year.

Typically, families will decorate their porch, or household shrine, don traditional Japanese clothing, eat ozōni and head out to a shrine or temple for hatsumōde, which means the first shrine visit of the year. It’s also common to rise early to watch the first sunrise of the year.



にがつ

February

nigatsu

In Japanese, February is called "Nigatsu" (二月). "Ni" means "two" and "gatsu" means "month", so February is literally "the second month" of the year.

February in Japan is still considered a cold month, but towards the end of the month, signs of spring start to appear, such as plum blossoms and early cherry blossoms. Valentine's Day is also celebrated on February 14th in Japan, but with a twist: traditionally, women give chocolates to men, and there are different types of chocolates depending on the relationship between the giver and receiver.



さんがつ

March

sangatsu

In Japanese, March is called "Sangatsu" (三月). "San" means "three" and "gatsu" means "month", so March is literally "the third month" of the year.

March is a time of change in Japan, as it marks the end of the academic and fiscal year for many businesses and schools. Graduation ceremonies for high school and university students take place in early March, followed by entrance ceremonies for new students at the beginning of April.

In terms of weather, March is a transitional month from winter to spring, and the cherry blossoms (sakura) start to bloom towards the end of the month in some parts of Japan.



しがつ

April

shigatsu

In Japan, the month of April is significant for several reasons. It marks the beginning of the fiscal year and academic year in Japan, and many companies and schools start their operations from April 1st. This period is called "Shin-Gakki" or "New School Year."

Another significant event that takes place in April in Japan is the arrival of spring and the blooming of cherry blossoms. This is a major cultural event in Japan, and people often gather in parks for "Hanami," which is the tradition of having picnics under the blooming cherry blossom trees.

Additionally, there are several public holidays in Japan in April, including Showa Day on April 29th, which commemorates the birthday of former Emperor Showa (Hirohito) and is a time for reflection on Japan's modern history.



ごがつ

May

gogatsu

May is a beautiful month to visit Japan, as the weather is mild and pleasant, and there are many cultural and seasonal events to enjoy. Here are a few things to look out for in May in Japan:

Golden Week: May 3rd to May 5th are public holidays in Japan, and many people take the week off work or school to travel or spend time with family. This period is called "Golden Week," and it's a busy time for transportation and tourist attractions.

Kodomo no Hi (Children's Day): May 5th is a national holiday in Japan, known as "Kodomo no Hi" or "Children's Day." Families with young boys often celebrate this day by hanging colorful carp-shaped flags called "koinobori" outside their homes.

Spring Festivals: May is a popular month for festivals in Japan, and many communities hold spring festivals known as "matsuri." These festivals feature food, games, music, and traditional performances, and are a great way to experience local culture.

Hiking and Nature: May is a great month to enjoy the outdoors in Japan, as the weather is mild and the scenery is beautiful. Many people go hiking in the mountains or visit parks and gardens to enjoy the spring flowers and greenery.



ろくがつ

June

rokugatsu

June is the beginning of summer in Japan, and it's a month filled with festivals, traditional events, and delicious seasonal foods. Here are some things to look out for in Japan in June:

Ajisai (Hydrangea) Festivals: Hydrangeas are in full bloom in June, and there are many festivals throughout Japan dedicated to these beautiful flowers. The most famous one is the Hakusan Shrine Hydrangea Festival in Tokyo, where you can see over 3,000 hydrangea plants in bloom.

Tsuyu (Rainy Season): June is the start of the rainy season in Japan, called "Tsuyu." Although it can be wet and humid, it's also a time when the countryside is lush and green, and there are fewer tourists.

Sanno Matsuri: The Sanno Matsuri is one of Tokyo's three major festivals, held every other year in June. It's a spectacular event with processions of traditional costumes, music, and portable shrines parading through the streets of Tokyo.

Seasonal Foods: June is a great month to enjoy seasonal foods in Japan, such as ayu (sweetfish), eel, and cold somen noodles. Many restaurants and markets offer special dishes featuring these fresh ingredients.

Beaches and Water Activities: With the start of summer, many people head to the beaches in Japan to swim, sunbathe, and enjoy water activities such as surfing, kayaking, and paddleboarding.



しちがつ

July

shichigatsu

July is a vibrant and festive month in Japan, with many traditional and cultural events taking place. Here are some things to look out for in Japan in July:

Tanabata Festival: The Tanabata Festival is a traditional festival held on July 7th, where people write their wishes on colorful pieces of paper and hang them on bamboo trees. The festival originated from a Chinese legend and is celebrated throughout Japan.

Gion Matsuri: Gion Matsuri is one of Japan's most famous festivals, held in Kyoto throughout July. It features processions of traditional floats, street food, and lively performances.

Fireworks Festivals: July is the month of fireworks festivals in Japan, with many spectacular displays taking place throughout the country. Some of the most famous ones include Sumida River Fireworks Festival in Tokyo and Tenjin Matsuri in Osaka.

Obon Festival: The Obon Festival is a traditional Japanese Buddhist event held in mid-July or August, where families welcome back the spirits of their ancestors. It's a time for reflection, offering food and prayers, and traditional Bon Odori dances.

Summer Foods: July is a great month to enjoy refreshing summer foods in Japan, such as shaved ice (kakigori), cold noodles (somen), and grilled meat (yakiniku). Many restaurants and street vendors offer seasonal dishes and drinks.



はちがつ

August

hachigatsu

August is one of the hottest and most exciting months in Japan, with many traditional festivals and events taking place throughout the country. Here are some things to look out for in Japan in August:

Obon Festival: The Obon Festival continues into August, with many communities holding traditional dances, rituals, and ceremonies to honor the spirits of their ancestors.

Fireworks Festivals: August is also a month of fireworks festivals in Japan, with many spectacular displays taking place throughout the country. Some of the most famous ones include the Sumida River Fireworks Festival in Tokyo and the Lake Biwa Fireworks Festival in Kyoto.

Summer Festivals: August is a popular month for summer festivals, or "matsuri," in Japan. These festivals feature food, games, music, and traditional performances, and are a great way to experience local culture. Some famous ones include the Awa Odori in Tokushima and the Nebuta Matsuri in Aomori.

Beaches and Water Activities: With the hot weather, many people head to the beaches in Japan to swim, sunbathe, and enjoy water activities such as surfing, kayaking, and paddleboarding.



くがつ

September

kugatsu

September is a great month to visit Japan as the weather starts to cool down, and the autumn leaves begin to turn in the northern regions. Here are some things to look out for in Japan in September:

Autumn Leaves: The autumn foliage season starts in the northern regions of Japan in September. Some of the best places to see autumn leaves include Hokkaido, Tohoku, and the Japanese Alps.

Harvest Festivals: September is also a time for harvest festivals in Japan, where communities celebrate the harvest season with traditional performances, music, and food. Some famous ones include the Kishiwada Danjiri Matsuri in Osaka and the Akimatsuri in Yokohama.

Sports Events: September is a big month for sports in Japan, with events such as the Tokyo Marathon and the All-Japan Kendo Championships taking place.

Food Festivals: September is a great month to enjoy seasonal foods in Japan, such as chestnuts, sweet potatoes, and mushrooms. Many restaurants and markets offer special dishes featuring these fresh ingredients.

Overall, September is a great month to experience the changing seasons and enjoy traditional cultural events and delicious seasonal foods in Japan.



じゅうがつ

October

juugatsu

In Japanese, October is called "juu-gatsu" (十月), which literally means "month ten" as it is the tenth month of the year. Here are some things to look out for in Japan in October:

Autumn Leaves: The autumn foliage season continues in October, with the leaves turning brilliant shades of red, orange, and yellow in many regions of Japan.

Halloween: Halloween has become increasingly popular in Japan in recent years, with many stores and restaurants offering Halloween-themed merchandise and food. Some neighborhoods, such as Shibuya in Tokyo, hold large street parties and costume contests.

Overall, October is a great month to experience the changing seasons and enjoy traditional cultural events and delicious seasonal foods in Japan.



じゅういちがつ

November

juuichigatsu

In Japanese, November is written as "十一月" (juuichigatsu) which literally means "month 11" (since "juu" means 10 and "ichi" means 1).

November is a great time to visit Japan, as the weather is generally mild and comfortable, with autumn foliage in full swing. Some popular events and activities during this month include viewing autumn leaves at gardens and parks, attending cultural festivals such as the Shichi-go-san (a traditional festival for children aged 3, 5, and 7), and enjoying seasonal foods like chestnuts, sweet potatoes, and grilled fish. It's also a great time to visit hot springs and enjoy the natural beauty of Japan's countryside.



じゅうにがつ

December

juunigatsu

In Japanese, December is written as "十二月" (juunigatsu) which literally means "month 12" (since "juu" means 10 and "ni" means 2).

December is a festive and lively month in Japan, as it marks the start of the winter season and the approach of the New Year. Some popular events and activities during this month include viewing winter illuminations at parks and shopping districts, attending traditional winter festivals such as the Sapporo Snow Festival, enjoying seasonal foods like hot pot dishes and mochi (a traditional rice cake), and participating in Hatsumode (the first shrine visit of the New Year). Christmas is also celebrated in Japan, with many people enjoying the festive decorations, exchanging gifts, and indulging in Christmas-themed foods and drinks.
